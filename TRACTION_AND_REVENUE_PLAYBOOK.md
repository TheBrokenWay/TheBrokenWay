# Traction and revenue playbook

This document is a practical playbook for how to get more attention, convert
that attention into trust, and convert trust into revenue without rebuilding
the wheel.

The emphasis here is not generic startup advice. It is pattern extraction from
companies and ecosystems that actually built adoption around technical products.

## Core conclusion

The repeatable formula is:

1. publish a clear public proof
2. make the first interaction extremely easy
3. repeat launches on a cadence
4. let community use the entry layer for free or cheaply
5. charge for trust, team control, compliance, and support

That is the common spine across the best examples.

## The observed pattern set

## 1. GitHub is the trust surface, not just the code host

GitHub's own docs emphasize that the profile README, repository README, topics,

- Social preview:
  https://docs.github.com/en/repositories/managing-your-repositorys-settings-and-features/customizing-your-repository/customizing-your-repositorys-social-media-preview
- Discussions:

Practical implication for you:

- GitHub is where strangers decide whether this is real

## 2. The strongest companies separate community access from paid trust layers

### Hugging Face

Hugging Face explicitly combines open community access with paid Team and
Enterprise offerings, including security, access controls, private artifacts,
and support.

Sources:

- homepage / enterprise framing:
  https://huggingface.co/

Pattern:

- broad public participation creates attention
- hosted convenience and enterprise controls create revenue

### Supabase

Supabase combines a generous public/developer entry surface with paid Pro,
Team, and Enterprise layers. Its pricing page highlights SSO, controls,
certifications, backups, and support in the higher tiers.

Sources:

- pricing:
  https://supabase.com/pricing
- billing docs:
  https://supabase.com/docs/guides/platform/billing-on-supabase

Pattern:

- easy adoption first
- team trust and compliance second

### HashiCorp

HashiCorp's public positioning shows a classic pattern: open tools create
adoption, while enterprises pay for coordination, governance, risk reduction,
and policy enforcement. Their Terraform Enterprise material specifically
highlights policy as code, coordination, and maintenance burden reduction.

Sources:

- Terraform open source vs enterprise:
  https://www.hashicorp.com/en/resources/why-consider-terraform-enterprise-over-open-source

Pattern:

- free engine for adoption
- governance and operational assurance for revenue

## 3. Repeated launch cadence matters more than one big reveal

Supabase repeatedly uses Launch Week as a distribution engine. It turns product
updates into an event, gives people a reason to return, and creates a reliable
rhythm of attention.

Sources:

- Launch Week 4:
  https://supabase.com/blog/supabase-launch-week-four
- Launch Week 8:
  https://supabase.com/launch-week/8
- Launch Week 12:
  https://supabase.com/launch-week/12

Pattern:

- regular launches beat long silence
- packaging matters, not just shipping
- the audience learns when to pay attention

## 4. Templates, examples, and proof artifacts shorten trust formation

Vercel/Next.js grew partly because people could see examples, deploy quickly,
and imagine themselves succeeding immediately.

Sources:

- Next.js on Vercel:
  https://vercel.com/frameworks/nextjs
- Next.js templates:
  https://vercel.com/templates/nextjs

Pattern:

- people adopt what they can picture using
- examples lower cognitive load
- proof beats explanation

## What this means for Tillar Technologies

You do not need a giant public release.

You need a structured ladder:

## Public layer

Goal:

- attention
- clarity
- credibility

Assets:

- GitHub profile
- CageOS Lite public demo
- publications shelf
- public lineage page
- short demos and screenshots
- founder thesis and product-family framing

## Operator layer

Goal:

- conversations
- pilots
- serious evaluation

Assets:

- private walkthrough
- redacted architecture deck
- decision-maker demo
- governed workflow examples
- operator-facing ROI framing

## Paid layer

Goal:

- revenue

Paid value should center on:

- deployment help
- governance configuration
- custom workflow mapping
- compliance and audit surfaces
- support and change management
- private validation and integration work

This matches the revenue logic visible in the examples above:
charge for trusted operation, not mere visibility.

## Specific recommendations for the next 30 days

## 1. Make the profile a deliberate funnel

Required outcome:

- stranger lands on GitHub profile
- understands thesis in under 20 seconds
- sees proof repo
- sees publications
- understands product-family split
- sees where to go next

Current direction:

- already underway

Next additions:

- pin the two public repos
- add a social preview image for the demo repo
- add one concise founder-proof timeline image

## 2. Turn CageOS Lite into an event surface

Do not leave it as a static repo.

Use a cadence:

- weekly or biweekly update notes
- one proof point per cycle
- one visible improvement per cycle
- one short distribution post per cycle

This is your version of a launch rhythm.

## 3. Build a private-commercial bridge

You need a clean sentence for the paid motion:

"The public repo proves the governance pattern. The paid offering applies that
pattern to real workflows, risk boundaries, and high-trust environments."

Without that bridge, attention does not naturally turn into revenue.

## 4. Sell the moat correctly

The moat is not "we also use AI."

The moat is:

- governance outside the model
- bounded operational control
- independent decision authority
- applicability across multiple high-trust workflows

That is much stronger positioning than feature lists.

## 5. Avoid the common trap

Do not wait for the full system to be complete before telling the story.

The successful pattern is:

- public proof now
- repeated visible improvement
- private enterprise depth behind the proof
